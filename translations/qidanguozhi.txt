**Revised Summary of the Fascicle's Contents (Incorporating the Burial, Posthumous Honors, Succession Crisis, Final Succession, and Authorial Commentary):**

This fascicle details the rise, reign, death, and legacy of Yelü Abaoji (Emperor Taizu), founder of the Liao Dynasty. It blends legendary origins, political history, cosmic portents, cultural-institutional creation, a graphic account of succession politics, and a final historiographical commentary to construct a foundational narrative for the Khitan state.

**1. The Making of a Khitan Unifier:**
*   Abaoji overthrows the old tribal rotational system, destroys rivals, and establishes sole rule.
*   He proclaims himself Emperor, founds a bureaucracy, and declares the state name "Khitan."
*   The text details his management of internal affairs, including a rebellion by his brother Sala Apuo and counsel from Empress Shulü.

**2. State-Building with Han Chinese Catalyst:**
*   The absorption of Han Chinese refugees and advisors (like Han Yanhui) is pivotal for developing administrative and economic structures.

**3. Exploiting the Central Plains' Chaos:**
*   The Khitan actively engage with the late Tang/Five Dynasties period through defections, diplomacy, and military campaigns.
*   Key events include a significant defeat by Li Cunxu's forces at Wangdu, witnessing the fall of the Later Liang and the rise/instability of the Later Tang, and coercive diplomacy following a Later Tang coup.

**4. Strategic Pivot and Regional Domination:**
*   **The Conquest of Bohai:** A critical strategic success where Abaoji diverts focus from the powerful Later Tang to conquer the Bohai kingdom. He establishes the Eastern Dan Kingdom as a vassal state, ruled by his eldest son, Tuyu (Yelü Bei).
*   **Settling the Succession:** Concurrently, Abaoji signals his second son, Deguang (future Emperor Taizong), as heir apparent by keeping him at the capital and granting him the title "Crown Prince Marshal."
*   **Sustained Southern Pressure:** Military pressure on the Later Tang frontier continues throughout.

**5. Cultural and Spatial Foundations of the State:**
*   **Script Creation:** Following the conquest of Bohai, Abaoji oversees the creation of the **Khitan script** (over 3,000 characters), a monumental act of cultural assertion and administrative necessity.
*   **Sacral and Spatial Order:** He establishes a Buddhist temple (**Tianxiong Temple**) at his main tribal base and institutes a **four-tower system** (Southern, Eastern, Northern, Western Towers) at key geographical points, including the capital (**Shangjing**, the Western Tower).
*   **Ritualized Movement:** The emperor's seasonal hunting itineraries cyclically connect these towers, physically enacting his dominion over this defined space.
*   **Ritual Orientation:** The east-facing orientation of city and palace gates reflects steppe cultural traditions and possibly cosmological beliefs.

**6. Cosmic Legitimacy and Foundational Myth:**
*   The story of Abaoji shooting a dragon serves as a potent omen, symbolizing his destined imperial power and foreshadowing the dynasty's geographical focus and eventual fall to the Jurchen (Jin).
*   A recorded **solar eclipse** reinforces this theme, embedding Liao history within a cosmic order and asserting the dynasty's authority to document Heaven's signs.

**7. The Founder's Death, Burial, and Deification:**
*   **Death in Bohai:** The fascicle reports the death of Emperor Taizu in **Fuyu City**, located within the recently conquered territory of Bohai, tying his demise to his greatest military achievement.
*   **Burial and Sacred Geography:** Taizu is buried at the sacred **Mount Muye**, the legendary Khitan ancestral homeland. The establishment of **Zuzhou (Ancestral Prefecture)** beside the tomb permanently administratively marks and guards this sacred imperial necropolis.
*   **Material and Cultic Legacy:** The text notes the contemporary existence of his temple and the preservation of his gigantic boots as sacred relics, indicating an ongoing cult of the founder.
*   **Posthumous Titles:** He receives the formal posthumous honorific **"Great Sage Emperor"** and the temple name **Taizu**, cementing his official status as the dynasty's revered progenitor.

**8. The Succession Crisis and the "Iron Empress Dowager":**
*   **Empress Shulü's Purge:** Following Taizu's death, Empress Dowager Shulü orchestrates a brutal purge to secure the succession for her chosen son, Deguang. She uses the pretext of sending messengers to the late emperor to execute hundreds of tribal chieftains and potential opponents.
*   **The Episode of Zhao Siwen:** The purge reaches a climax when the Han official **Zhao Siwen** from **Pingzhou** refuses to go. His clever retort forces the Empress Dowager's hand. To maintain her authority and the ritual pretext without sacrificing herself, she performs a shocking act of self-mutilation, **severing her own wrist** to be buried in Taizu's tomb in her stead. This act, earning her the nickname "the Iron Empress Dowager," underscores the violent realities of the dynastic transition and her formidable, ruthless political will.

**9. The Final Succession:**
*   **Enthronement of Deguang:** Following the purge and the consolidation of power by Empress Dowager Shulü, the fascicle concludes with the formal succession: **Yelü Deguang** is enthroned as the new emperor. This act completes the dynastic transition, fulfilling the late Taizu's earlier indications and his mother's bloody machinations, and establishes the reign of Emperor Taizong of Liao.

**10. Authorial Commentary ("Lun Yue"):**
*   The fascicle concludes with a formal historiographical comment. It places the Khitan (Liao) within the long history of "barbarian" interactions with China, comparing Abaoji's rise to that of earlier non-Han dynasties like the Tuoba Wei.
*   It acknowledges Abaoji's exceptional prowess and the historical conditions (the collapse of the Tang) that enabled his success, while simultaneously expressing a traditional Chinese historiographical bias by describing the Khitan's nature as "human exterior but a beastly interior."
*   This commentary grapples with the tension between attributing historical change to "human affairs" (Abaoji's extraordinary ability) and "destiny of fortune" (*yùn shù*), ultimately framing the Liao's foundation as a formidable, heaven-sanctioned, yet morally ambiguous event within the broader cycles of Central Plains history.

**Overall Fascicle Theme:**
The text constructs the Liao's foundational myth. It charts Abaoji's transformation from tribal chieftain to emperor and then to deified ancestor. This is achieved through **internal political mastery**, **strategic adaptation**, **institution-building**, and the **cultivation of cosmic and ancestral legitimacy**. The narrative arc moves from the **conquest of Bohai**, through profound **cultural creation**, to the **founder's death and burial within a sacred and administrative landscape**. The final acts of **bestowing posthumous titles** and **establishing a guarded tomb and cult** formally conclude the process of founding a dynasty. The subsequent **purge, the wrist-severing episode, and the final enthronement** provide a critical coda, highlighting the brutal, personal, and often contingent politics required to secure the founder's legacy. Finally, the **authorial commentary** steps back to contextualize this entire Khitan project within the grand, and often tragic, narrative of Chinese history, judging it as a phenomenon of both formidable human agency and inscrutable historical fate.
